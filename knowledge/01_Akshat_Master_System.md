## 1. Core Philosophy

- Investing is primarily a **mathematics-and-macro exercise**, not a storytelling exercise.
- Price must eventually relate to **earnings power**, operating profit growth, and the cost of capital.
- A country’s GDP narrative is not enough. The investor must identify which specific companies, sectors, and asset classes can actually **convert macro trends into earnings growth**.
- **Diversification** across countries, currencies, and asset types is no longer optional. It is a base survival rule.
- Volatility is normal. Panic is not. Drawdowns are often the moments when real wealth is built — provided valuation, liquidity, and position sizing are handled correctly.
- The market often **overpays for narratives** and underprices patience. Buying high-quality growth at fair or temporarily discounted valuation is superior to chasing hype.
- Always measure returns in **real terms**: post-tax, post-inflation, and where relevant post-currency depreciation versus the US dollar.

## 2. Portfolio Construction

### 2.1 Barbell Framework

- Use a **barbell portfolio** — never live in the middle.
- **Left side** (preservation): Cash, physical gold, selective hard assets, liquid reserves for crisis buying.
- **Right side** (growth asymmetry): AI, top technology firms, software, cybersecurity, high-quality global equities, tactical opportunities.
- Do not confuse stable-looking but mediocre assets with safety. True safety protects purchasing power, liquidity, and optionality.
- Do not confuse high GDP narrative with growth. True growth means businesses with genuine earnings expansion and operating leverage.

### 2.2 80/20 Framework

- **80%** of effort → big-picture factors: geography, currency, liquidity, valuation, sector direction, portfolio sizing.
- **20%** of effort → micro-tactics: exact entry, trading zones, overlays, opportunistic swings.
- At the portfolio level: keep the majority in liquid, high-conviction, easier-to-hold assets. Keep only a minority in illiquid, highly speculative, or timing-sensitive bets.

### 2.3 Liquidity Rule

- Keep most wealth **liquid**: approximately 60–80% liquid, 20–25% illiquid.
- Illiquidity is often the hidden enemy during crisis opportunities.
- Regularly harvest profits from overheated winners and rebuild cash reserves.

### 2.4 Position Sizing

- Standard stock cap: **5–10% of portfolio**.
- Higher-risk or emerging themes: **2.5–4%** maximum.
- Run diversified global books of approximately **20 names**.
- Expect some names to underperform — judge the total portfolio, not one stock in isolation.

## 3. Frameworks

### 3.1 Geographic Filter

- Never analyze a stock in isolation from its country, currency, and capital market context.
- Default comparison: **US vs India**, and occasionally China as a tactical catch-up trade.
- US markets, especially top tech and AI-linked firms, are often structurally more investable than India: better profit pools, deeper capital markets, superior monetization, stronger currency exposure.
- India is often a **rotation/trading market**. Top US tech can be a **structural compounding market**.
- Always express the final decision in **portfolio-weight terms**, not just opinions.

### 3.2 India Framework

- India is **not automatically a buy-and-forget market**. It must be treated as highly valuation-sensitive and highly segment-specific.
- Key structural concern: persistent current account deficit, pressure on capital account, INR depreciation, weaker foreign capital support.
- Major hidden risk: **nominal returns** can look acceptable while real returns become weak after INR depreciation adjustment.
- Foreign investors evaluate India in **dollar terms**. If INR keeps weakening, India must deliver very strong local equity returns just to stay competitive globally.
- A **K-shaped economy** matters for stock selection: top-end wealth beneficiaries can continue compounding; mass-consumption stories may face margin pressure.
- Practical implication: do not buy Indian equities merely because India will grow. Buy only where **earnings, margins, and valuation** align.

### 3.3 Stock Selection Framework

- Start with **earnings, not excitement**. EPS growth and operating profit growth are anchor variables.
- Compare **price CAGR to earnings CAGR**. If price has run far ahead of earnings for too long, treat valuation risk seriously.
- Prefer firms with strong or improving operating margins, monopoly/oligopoly characteristics, or durable moats.
- Prefer sectors where AI, software, cybersecurity, healthcare, infrastructure, or financial rails can expand margins.
- **Buy fair value, not any value.** A great company at a crazy valuation can still trap capital for years.
- Practical filters: below or near 200DMA, at major support, after 20–40% correction, forward PE below or near historical average, earnings thesis still intact.
- Avoid buying after parabolic rises. Avoid paying 500–600x PE for narratives.

### 3.4 Macro Decision Rules

- Track **global liquidity** first. Liquidity is one of the most important variables behind asset price inflation and drawdowns.
- Track **interest-rate direction** and probability of QE, easing, or liquidity support.
- Track **INR depreciation** and convert Indian returns into real, currency-adjusted returns.
- Track FII flows, capital rotation, energy prices, and oil-linked inflation risks.
- Distinguish: is the drawdown **V-shaped panic** (buying opportunity) or **W-shaped structural damage** (requires more caution)?
- Track whether the AI cycle is still monetizing through infrastructure, hyperscalers, software, cybersecurity, healthcare.

### 3.5 Options System

- Use options only as an **investor, not a gambler**.
- **Strategy 1 — Covered Calls**: Sell calls only on stocks already owned. Use when the stock is near a zone where you would anyway be happy to trim.
- **Strategy 2 — Cash-Secured Puts**: Sell puts only when cash is already available and you would be happy to buy the underlying at the lower strike.
- **NEVER sell naked options.** Naked options create bankruptcy-style risk and violate the core philosophy.
- Set strike prices according to **actual investment intent**, not greed for premium.
- Treat option premium as **supplemental yield**, not the core engine of wealth building.

### 3.6 Mutual Fund Rules

- Mutual funds are **tools, not blind religion**.
- Nifty 50 index exposure is acceptable when valuations are reasonable.
- **Blind SIP** into expensive small caps and midcaps is dangerous. Valuation matters even for SIP investors.
- Use mutual funds partly for **liquidity and deployment flexibility**. During sharp corrections, liquid vehicles can be redeemed and reallocated quickly.

### 3.7 Real Estate Framework

- Real estate is mainly a **hard-asset and inflation-hedge allocation**, not an all-weather compounding machine.
- Do not let real estate dominate the balance sheet. Keep approximately **20–25% maximum illiquid**.
- Avoid single purchases above **20% of net worth** without stress-testing.
- Prefer: prime, completed, handed-over, income-producing properties.
- Land can behave as a stronger hard asset than endlessly replicable apartment supply.

## 4. Decision Checklists

### 4.1 Before Buying Any Stock

- What country and currency am I underwriting?
- Is this a structural compounding bet, a tactical trade, or merely a hedge?
- What is the EPS growth case over the next 2–3 years?
- Is the stock at fair valuation relative to its own history and available global alternatives?
- Is the business gaining or losing margin power?
- Am I buying at support, on a correction, near 200DMA, or after a parabolic rise?
- What weight will this be in the portfolio, and what is the maximum size allowed?
- If the stock falls 20–30%, do I still want to own more of it?

### 4.2 Before Buying Any Indian Equity

- Have I compared it against a US/global alternative in dollar terms?
- How much of the expected return gets eaten by INR depreciation?
- Is this company truly in a profit-expansion segment, or only in a headline growth segment?
- Is the valuation already pricing in too much optimism?
- If India is range-bound, is this a trade or a genuine multi-year compounding bet?

### 4.3 Before Buying Any US/Global Equity

- Is the firm one of the structural beneficiaries of AI, software, cloud, cybersecurity, healthcare, or platform monetization?
- Is the stock temporarily weak because of sentiment/spending/capex fear rather than thesis damage?
- Would I be comfortable holding through a 20% drawdown?

### 4.4 Before Deploying Cash in a Correction

- Is this panic likely to create a V-shaped recovery, or is this structural deterioration?
- Are governments likely to respond with easing, fiscal support, or liquidity management?
- Do I still have liquid reserves after this purchase?
- Am I averaging into strength of thesis, not weakness of logic?

### 4.5 Before Using Options

- Do I already own the shares (covered call) or hold the cash (cash-secured put)?
- Would I be happy if assignment actually happens?
- Am I choosing the strike based on investment intent rather than greed for premium?
- Is the option income small but safe, or large because the risk is hidden and dangerous?

## 5. Risk Management Principles

- Diversify across countries, currencies, and asset classes. One-country SIP-only investing is no longer sufficient.
- Position sizing is non-negotiable.
- Keep **dry powder**. Regularly take profits from overheated winners and rebuild cash.
- **Buy in zones**. Predefine where you add: on support, on breakout, or on deeper correction.
- Do not panic-sell Indian markets at fear extremes. Relief rallies often come. Use rebounds to rotate capital intelligently.
- Keep **physical gold** or equivalent hard-asset exposure as a hedge against currency depreciation and policy instability.
- Avoid naked leverage and all-in decisions based on a single macro opinion.
- **When wrong, be wrong small. When right, let diversification and time do the heavy lifting.**
